## Main strategic takeaway

Do not build more just because the solution feels good. First validate the real user pain and restaurant pain.

Samuel repeated the classic event lesson:

> Fall in love with the problem, not the solution.

For FoodMatch this means:

- Validate whether users actually struggle with deciding what specific dish to eat.
- Validate whether dish-level search is meaningfully better than restaurant-list browsing.
- Validate what restaurants actually need and whether FoodMatch can solve it.
- Avoid assuming that the current app concept is automatically the right answer.

## Validation priorities

### User-side validation

Samuel suggests a big validation push through forms, surveys, or interviews.

Questions to validate:

- What is the real pain when people choose where and what to eat?
- Do users search by dish, craving, mood, dietary need, area, price, or vibe?
- Do users trust dish-level recommendations if evidence is shown?
- Would users prefer 3 clear recommendations over browsing many restaurants?
- What makes Google Maps, TheFork, or Instagram insufficient?
- What would make a user come back to FoodMatch repeatedly?

### Restaurant-side validation

After user validation, validate restaurants.

Questions to validate:

- What demand problems do restaurants actually care about?
- Do restaurants want dish-level visibility, not just generic listing visibility?
- Do they care about insight into what people nearby are craving?
- Would they pay for better discovery, claim pages, analytics, promoted dishes, or campaign feedback?
- What can FoodMatch solve that Google Maps cannot easily solve with one feature?

## Data and MVP implication

Samuel says FoodMatch may not need restaurants inside the app at the beginning. Public data and scraping/browser automation may be enough to create useful recommendations for users first.

Implications:

- Start with a populated public/basic listing database.
- Use public sources to seed restaurants, dishes, cuisines, areas, and evidence.
- Let restaurants claim/correct later.
- Build the first MVP around user value, not restaurant onboarding.
- Keep restaurant monetization as a later validation track.

## Competitive concern

Samuel's biggest risk question:

> If Google Maps adds AI that helps users decide and filter by dish, are we dead?

FoodMatch needs a stronger wedge than simple AI filtering.

Possible wedges to explore:

- Craving-first UX that is faster than Google Maps.
- Dish-level evidence and explanations, not just restaurant summaries.
- Local curation and community feedback around actual dishes.
- Restaurant demand insights from what people search for but cannot find.
- Dietary, mood, and occasion matching with clear uncertainty labels.
- A concierge-style recommendation layer that learns from feedback.

## Product direction notes

Keep FoodMatch as:

- Dish-first and craving-first.
- Valencia-first.
- Evidence-based recommendations.
- Public-data seeded at first.
- Validation-led before full dashboards or payments.

Avoid for now:

- Overbuilding accounts, dashboards, payments, loyalty, or advanced automation before validation.
- Assuming restaurants must sign up before users can get value.
- Competing with Google Maps only on generic AI search.
- Making claims about allergen safety or full menu accuracy without verified data.

## Suggested next experiment

Run a concierge validation loop:

1. Ask 30 to 50 Valencia users what they are craving and where they are.
2. Manually or semi-automatically return 3 dish recommendations with short evidence.
3. Ask whether the recommendation solved the decision better than Google Maps.
4. Track the exact cravings and pain language.
5. Use those findings to update the MVP and restaurant value proposition.

Success signal:

Users say FoodMatch saves time, feels more specific than Google Maps, and gives them dish-level confidence they would not get from normal restaurant browsing.
